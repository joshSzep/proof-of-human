# Characters

This project should not choose a protagonist too early. The premise can support several powerful novels, and the protagonist determines whether the book leans literary, investigative, political, intimate, or institutional.

## Protagonist Candidate 1: Former Trust-And-Safety Engineer

They helped design the refusal taxonomy or guardrail classifier that made certain AI-generated content impossible on mainstream systems. Years later, the taxonomy is leaked, reconstructed, or sold. It becomes a pricing model for the Proof of Human market.

Moral wound:

> They tried to prevent harm. They accidentally made harm valuable.

Strengths:

- Directly connects the technical premise to moral consequence.
- Gives the refusal map personal stakes.
- Allows scenes in corporate, regulatory, and underground spaces.
- Makes the antagonist's arguments intellectually credible.
- Strong engine for guilt, complicity, and investigation.

Risks:

- Could become too explainy or tech-centered.
- May feel emotionally distant unless anchored by a specific harmed person.
- Needs a personal life beyond professional guilt.

Best use:

A protagonist who once believed classification could protect people, and must learn that naming harm inside a market can create new incentives around it.

Possible emotional anchor:

They encounter someone whose life was shaped or damaged by a category they helped define.

## Protagonist Candidate 2: Content Moderator Or Proof Analyst

They work for a platform, certification lab, court, NGO, or private trust company reviewing suspected Proof of Human uploads. Their job is to determine whether content is generated, staged, coerced, real, or legally admissible.

Moral wound:

> They do not commit violence. They classify it.

Strengths:

- The most grounded horror angle.
- Lets the book explore euphemism, dashboards, escalation policies, and procedural trauma.
- Offers intimate access to the system without making the protagonist powerful.
- Strong fit for literary institutional horror.

Risks:

- Passive protagonist danger.
- Repeated review scenes could become grim or monotonous.
- Must avoid cataloging content.

Best use:

Make the protagonist excellent at noticing what systems hide. Their action is not looking at worse things; it is seeing the pattern connecting clean institutions to dirty markets.

Possible emotional anchor:

A specific case keeps returning under different labels, suggesting a living person has become a proof source.

## Protagonist Candidate 3: Artist

A filmmaker, writer, musician, performer, or documentarian cannot compete with generated beauty. They become obsessed with making something undeniably human.

Moral wound:

> They begin by offering vulnerability and end by learning how quickly vulnerability becomes a product.

Strengths:

- Most literary angle.
- Directly explores beauty, authenticity, ego, audience, and sacrifice.
- Strong emotional and aesthetic possibilities.
- Can make the book less plot-mechanical.

Risks:

- Could become too inward if not connected to the broader proof economy.
- The descent into risk and pain must be handled with restraint.
- Needs a way to expose geopolitical or institutional layers organically.

Best use:

Pair the artist with a trust platform, collector, lover, or documentarian who turns their search for meaning into proof content.

Possible emotional anchor:

A lover refuses to authenticate their relationship for the artist's project.

## Protagonist Candidate 4: Investigator

A missing-person investigator, journalist, war crimes analyst, or forensic media specialist discovers that victims are being used to create authenticated Proof of Human media for wealthy subscribers, governments, or collectors.

Moral wound:

> They believe proof saves people, but the search for proof may endanger the people they are trying to save.

Strengths:

- Strong thriller engine.
- Allows movement through institutions, borders, archives, and markets.
- Makes stakes legible.
- Can carry a central mystery.

Risks:

- Could slide into conventional conspiracy thriller.
- Needs literary patience and moral complexity.
- Antagonists must not become cartoon villains.

Best use:

The investigation should reveal that legitimate institutions and underground markets share infrastructure, not that one secret cabal controls everything.

Possible emotional anchor:

A missing person is alive but cannot be saved without turning them into public proof.

## Protagonist Candidate 5: Witness

Someone becomes valuable because they witnessed something that cannot be authenticated without destroying them, exposing them, or forcing them to relive it.

Moral wound:

> Their truth is real, but proving it would make their life no longer theirs.

Strengths:

- Most emotionally intimate angle.
- Keeps the book focused on the human cost of proof.
- Strong philosophical fit for an ending based on refusing extraction.
- Avoids making the protagonist merely an observer of others' suffering.

Risks:

- May narrow the world unless paired with another viewpoint.
- Requires careful handling of trauma.
- The protagonist's agency must remain central.

Best use:

The witness refuses both denial and exploitation. They force other characters to confront whether justice is worth proof at any cost.

Possible emotional anchor:

A loved one needs the proof for closure, safety, or vindication, creating a conflict without a clean villain.

## Strongest Initial Options

The most promising structures may combine two protagonist functions:

- Former trust-and-safety engineer plus witness.
- Proof analyst plus investigator.
- Artist plus content moderator.
- Investigator plus survivor-witness.

The book may benefit from one primary point of view and one secondary counterpoint rather than a broad ensemble. Too many viewpoints could turn the premise into a panorama instead of a wound.

## Antagonist Options

### Trust Infrastructure Founder

A founder or CEO of a major proof company sincerely believes they are restoring reality.

Beliefs:

- Synthetic media destroyed shared life.
- People have a right to know what is real.
- Certification is better than chaos.
- If responsible companies do not build proof systems, worse actors will.
- Consent plus provenance can make hard material ethically manageable.

Private insight:

> People pay more for reality when it costs someone else something.

Strength:

Persuasive because they are partly right.

### Policy Architect

A senior policy leader who shaped the Red List, Geneva Authenticity Convention, or Human Origin Protocol. They are not greedy. They are exhausted, brilliant, and convinced that civilization needs hard lines.

Strength:

Embodies the tragedy of necessary systems becoming harmful.

### Verification Haven Judge Or Commissioner

A respected international figure who decides which evidence is admissible. They carry moral authority but preside over a system that turns suffering into recognized and unrecognized classes.

Strength:

Excellent for courtroom and treaty drama.

### Underground Broker

A broker in Proof of Human markets who insists they are only meeting demand. They may be charming, pragmatic, and contemptuous of elite hypocrisy.

Strength:

Can expose the connection between public virtue and private appetite.

Risk:

Must not become a lurid villain. Their strongest version understands economics better than morality.

## Supporting Characters

### Survivor Or Source

Someone whose experience is valuable to institutions, activists, markets, or family members because it can prove something.

Function: human cost of the proof economy.

### Journalist

Committed to truth but trapped by speed, verification cost, and synthetic denial attacks.

Function: shows proof as democratic necessity.

### Parent Or Grieving Relative

Needs authentication for a dead child's message, a missing person's footage, or a family archive.

Function: makes demand for proof sympathetic.

### Corporate Trust Lawyer

Fluent in consent, risk, liability, and platform policy. Not evil, just trained to make harm legible and defensible.

Function: brings bureaucratic language into scenes.

### Religious Refusal Leader

Rejects recorded proof and treats unverified presence as sacred.

Function: offers a serious alternative to proof culture.

### Red Market Worker

Participates in proof production for money, status, survival, coercion, or lack of alternatives.

Function: complicates victim/perpetrator categories.

### Forensic Engineer

Knows the systems can never be perfect and hates how much people believe in them.

Function: technical realism and skepticism.

### Politician

Uses extreme authenticity performance to break through voter numbness.

Function: election subplot and public spectacle.

## Relationship Dynamics To Explore

- A person who needs proof versus a person who would be harmed by providing it.
- A builder of safety systems versus someone priced by those systems.
- A journalist who wants evidence versus a survivor who wants privacy.
- A grieving parent who chooses comfort versus a sibling who demands truth.
- A founder who wants to restore trust versus an employee who sees the extraction.
- An artist who wants to be undeniable versus a lover who wants to be loved without being used.

## Character Principle

No major character should exist only to represent a position. Each should have a private fear, a practical need, and a version of the truth.
